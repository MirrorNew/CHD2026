Automatically optimizing heuristics 
for robust scale-free network 
design via large language models
He Yu1,2 & Jing Liu1,2
Achieving scale-free robust networks is a challenging problem due to its NP-hard nature and the vast, 
complex, high-dimensional solution space. Current methods, from handcrafted feature extraction 
to deep learning approaches, have made certain advancements but remain rigid and complex, 
often requiring manual design, trial and error, and large amounts of labeled data. To address these 
challenges, we propose AutoRNet, a novel framework that integrates large language models (LLMs) 
with evolutionary algorithms to generate complete heuristics for robust network design, supported by 
expert-crafted Network Optimization Strategies (NOSs). With the intrinsic properties of robust scale-
free network structures in mind, NOS-based variation operations provide domain-specific prompts 
for LLMs, leveraging expert-defined domain knowledge to guide the creation of advanced heuristics. 
Moreover, to address the difficulty posed by the hard constraint of maintaining degree distributions, an 
adaptive fitness function is designed, progressively strengthening constraints to balance convergence 
and diversity. We evaluate the robustness of networks generated by AutoRNet’s heuristics on both 
sparse and dense initial scale-free networks. These solutions outperform those from current methods. 
AutoRNet reduces the extent of manual heuristic design by utilizing structured domain knowledge 
through expert-crafted NOSs, offering a flexible and adaptive approach for generating robust scale-
free network structures. The source code and dataset for this work have been made publicly available 
at https://github.com/leonyuhe/AutoRNet.
Keywords Evolutionary algorithms, Large language models, Complex networks, Network robustness, 
Prompt engineering, Deep learning
Modern networked systems, particularly scale-free networks characterized by power-law degree distributions, 
form the backbone of contemporary society1–3. These networks, prevalent in real-world systems such as social, 
biological, and internet infrastructures, exhibit unique robustness properties, including resilience to random 
failures but vulnerability to targeted attacks 4. Improving the robustness of scale-free networks to prevent 
catastrophic disruptions is inherently challenging due to the NP-hard nature of the problem and the complex, 
high-dimensional solution space. Although research in this area has made significant advancements, key 
contributions include approximate theoretical models5, which simplify the complex interactions within scale-
free networks to provide insights for enhancing resilience. Optimization algorithms, employing approaches such 
as simulated annealing (SA)6, genetic algorithms (GAs)7, and greedy approaches8, offer near-optimal solutions 
in a reasonable time frame. In addition, machine learning techniques, particularly deep reinforcement learning9, 
have been used to adapt dynamically and improve network configurations. However, these methods depend 
greatly on manual work, expert knowledge, training data, and trial-and-error processes.
The emergence of coding-oriented large language models (LLMs) 10 has garnered significant attention 
because of their potential in addressing combinatorial optimization problems 11,12. FunSearch 13 combines a 
pretrained LLM with evolutionary algorithms (EAs) to evolve initial low-scoring programs into high-scoring 
ones. Evolution of heuristics (EoH) 14 is applied to evolve heuristic descriptions and their corresponding code 
implementations together. However, both FunSearch and EoH depend primarily on existing optimization 
algorithms, and only scoring or weighting mechanisms that influence the search algorithm behavior are designed 
by LLMs to guide the related operations without creating a new algorithm. This limits the applicability of these 
methods to complex domain-specific problems, such as ensuring network robustness. Therefore, there is a need 
to generate whole algorithms directly rather than merely modifying data through weighting and scoring.

In this paper, we address these issues and make a significant contribution by proposing AutoRNet, an integrated 
framework that combines the contextual intelligence of LLMs with the adaptive optimization capabilities of EAs. 
Specifically, we design network optimization strategy (NOS)-based variation operations tailored for complex 
network problems, which can create domain-specific prompts for LLMs, generating a variety of heuristics that 
are suitable for different network-related challenges. Moreover, we design an adaptive fitness function (AFF) to 
evaluate these heuristics, progressively tightening the constraints to balance convergence and diversity, thereby 
obtaining superior heuristics. AutoRNet leverages special network characteristics, through expert-crafted NOSs, 
to guide the design of effective operations. The major contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
• A hybrid framework is developed, in which EAs and LLMs iteratively collaborate to generate and refine heu-
ristics for optimizing network robustness.
• NOS-based variation operations are designed, utilizing expert-defined prompts to guide LLMs in divergent 
thinking. These NOSs are equally applicable to other network-related challenges.
• An AFF tailored to network issues is designed to transform hard constraints into soft constraints, increasing 
the diversity and resilience of heuristics.
• The solutions generated by AutoRNet’s heuristics are evaluated across eight scale-free networks with varying 
sizes and densities and a real-world network, and it is shown that they outperform current methods.

Related work
Related work on improving network robustness
Traditional methods for improving network robustness focus on improving local redundancy and connectivity, 
such as by maximizing the clustering coefficient to form tightly knit groups of nodes. These methods also leverage 
high-order network structures such as triangles and quadrilaterals to identify and protect critical nodes and edges 
to withstand targeted attacks. Techniques such as greedy algorithms and local search algorithms systematically 
increase network robustness. Chan et al. 15 introduced a local search algorithm by iteratively improving the 
subgraph structure to improve the robustness of the network.
Metaheuristic algorithms, including genetic algorithms (GAs) 16 and simulated annealing (SA)17, have also 
been extensively employed to solve network robustness problems. Pizzuti et al. 18 proposed using GAs to 
enhance the robustness of complex networks by simulating the process of natural selection to evolve network 
configurations over generations, improving resilience against failures and attacks. Zhou et al.7 proposed the use 
of memetic algorithms, which combine global and local search strategies, to increase the robustness of scale-free 
networks by integrating global and local search operators. Buesser et al.6 proposed the use of SA to optimize the 
robustness of scale-free networks by rewiring the network edges, thus improving the resilience of the network to 
fragmentation and intentional damage. 
However, these methods often require manual design and multiple trial-and-error processes, making them 
inefficient and time-consuming. The need for extensive experimentation and tuning reduces their practicality 
in large-scale and dynamic network environments, highlighting the need for more automated and adaptive 
approaches to network robustness optimization.
Deep learning, particularly graph neural networks (GNNs) , has emerged as a powerful approach for 
addressing network robustness problems. Tang et al.19 explored methods of increasing the robustness of GNNs 
against poisoning attacks by leveraging clean graphs from similar domains. This approach helps the GNNs detect 
adversarial edges more effectively, enhancing their resistance to attacks. Wang et al.20 developed certifiably robust 
GNNs to defend against attacks that perturb the graph structure by adding or deleting edges. Their approach 
ensures that the performance of the GNNs remains stable even under adversarial conditions.
Despite their strengths, the above approaches face significant challenges. Traditional and metaheuristic 
methods often require manual design and extensive trial-and-error, making them inefficient for large-scale or 
dynamic networks. Although GNN-based methods of increasing network robustness are powerful, they require 
large amounts of labeled data for training, which can be difficult to obtain in practice. The high computational 
demand of training and inference is also a limitation, especially in real-time applications. Moreover, GNNs can 
still be vulnerable to sophisticated adversarial attacks, where small, carefully crafted perturbations in the input 
data can lead to incorrect classifications. These limitations underscore the need for automated, adaptive methods 
like AutoRNet to optimize network robustness efficiently.
Application of LLMs in combinatorial optimization
LLMs have had a significant impact on addressing various problems. One prevalent approach involves 
engineering in-context learning prompts (EILP)21 via techniques such as zero-shot, few-shot, and chain-of-thought 
(CoT) prompting22–24. Enhancing EILP with fine-tuning further increases the response accuracy by adapting 
LLMs to specific datasets or tasks. Furthermore, combining EILP with ensemble learning techniques increases 
robustness and consistency by aggregating multiple model predictions. Integration with retrieval-augmented 
generation (RAG) 25 enables the use of external knowledge sources, allowing LLMs to generate more accurate 
and informed contextual responses.
Many researchers have also focused on solving combinatorial optimization problems via LLMs. Liu et al. 26 
presented the first investigation into LLMs as evolutionary combination optimizers for solving the travelling

discover new constructions and improve existing solutions by iteratively refining the function constructions. 
EoH14 facilitates the simultaneous evolution of concept descriptions and code implementations, emulating the 
process by which humans develop heuristics to achieve efficient automatic heuristic design. These approaches 
have demonstrated competitive performance compared with that of traditional heuristics in finding high-quality

All of these methods are based on existing algorithms; scoring or weighting mechanisms are used to modify 
the original data (e.g., bin capacities, city distances), influencing the search algorithm behaviour without creating 
a new algorithm. There is a need to generate whole algorithms directly rather than merely modifying data through 
weighting and scoring. The optimization improvements of these approaches are restricted to relatively simple 
combinatorial problems, and they cannot be extended to more complex or domain-specific optimization scenarios.
Unlike FunSearch, which evolves scoring functions within predefined algorithmic skeletons, and EoH, which 
co-evolves heuristic descriptions and code for simpler combinatorial problems, AutoRNet generates complete 
heuristics tailored to the domain-specific challenge of network robustness. By integrating NOSs into LLM 
prompts, AutoRNet leverages intrinsic network properties like degree distributions and centrality measures 
to guide heuristic design. Furthermore, AutoRNet’s AFF dynamically balances hard constraints, overcoming 
the limitations of rigid constraint enforcement and enhancing heuristic diversity and effectiveness. Benefiting 
from the NOS and AFF , AutoRNet can design sophisticated algorithms that generalize across diverse network 
topologies, outperforming traditional and prior LLM-based methods in synthetic and real-world scenarios.

of edges. To measure network robustness, several methodologies have been developed. Literature 3 introduced 
a measure R to assess network robustness by evaluating the size of the largest connected component during

Complex networks can exhibit different topological structures, with random and scale-free networks being 
two of the most studied types. Random networks, characterized by a homogeneous degree distribution, are 
robust to targeted attacks on high-degree nodes. In contrast, scale-free networks, with a power-law degree 
distribution, exhibit exceptional robustness against random failures due to the low probability of removing a 
hub but are extremely susceptible to targeted attacks that focus on their few high-degree hubs. These networks 
have distinct characteristics that significantly impact their robustness and vulnerability to failures or attacks.
In this paper, we use R to evaluate network robustness, specifically focusing on targeted attacks through the 
sequential removal of the highest-degree nodes. We employ scale-free networks for both training and testing 
graph sets to accurately reflect the structures of many real-world networks.

AutoRNet initializes its heuristic population by providing LLMs with a detailed task specification that includes 
a description of the problem, function signature, and functionality of the heuristic. The LLM generates the initial 
heuristics by creating and implementing them as Python code, with the process repeated multiple times to 
ensure diversity. Optionally, heuristic seed codes , either simple or expert-designed, are included to guide the 
LLM towards effective methods. Existing hand-crafted heuristics can also be incorporated to enhance the initial 
population with additional domain knowledge. The detailed population initialization prompts are provided in 
Appendix A.
Adaptive fitness function
When using an EA to assist LLMs in heuristic code generation, individuals are encoded as heuristics—methods 
that can generate solutions rather than problem solutions—operating in a distinct search space with a mapping 
to solution fitness, and the search is based on LLMs that generate new or similar methods. This does not inherently 
provide a mechanism for measuring the similarity between methods, making it difficult to navigate the method 
space. Maintaining a consistent degree distribution is a constraint in optimizing network robustness. Strictly 
enforcing this constraint from the outset can be overly restrictive, leading to many invalid individuals and 
thereby impeding the evolutionary process.
To address these issues, we design an adaptive fitness function (AFF), which can dynamically adjust the degree 
distribution constraint throughout the evolutionary process. The AFF initially relaxes this constraint, facilitating 
broader exploration of the method space, and then progressively tightens this constraint as the evolution 
continues. In traditional EAs, individuals represent problem solutions, and selection methods like roulette wheel 
or tournament selection preserve diversity by probabilistically including suboptimal solutions based on their 
fitness. AFF relaxes constraints directly on heuristics during selection, enabling a diverse heuristic population to 
evolve. Over generations, these constraints tighten to ensure valid, robust solutions, offering a valuable approach 
to designing effective heuristics for complex network optimization.

Evolutionary impact
The AFF’s adaptive process, starting with relaxed constraints and progressively increasing penalties via 
w (t), fundamentally enhances AutoRNet’ s ability to generate a diverse set of heuristics. Early in the evolution, 
minimal penalties allow heuristics with varying structural deviations to thrive, fostering exploration across 
the heuristic space and preventing premature convergence to local optima. As w (t) grows, the AFF guides 
selection toward heuristics that meet constraints, achieving maximum fitness (2 × R( ˜Gt,i,j)) when deviations 
(Ddiﬀ = Ediﬀ =0 ) are eliminated. This dynamic adjustment ensures a rich diversity of heuristics initially, 
which is critical for discovering innovative strategies, while later refining them into robust, valid solutions for 
diverse network topologies.
Network optimization strategy-based variation operations
The heuristic-method search space for EAs assisting LLMs in heuristic code generation is vast, complex, and 
high-dimensional. In simpler problem domains, such as those addressed by EoH and FunSearch, this space 
is reduced by limiting function codes to straightforward tasks such as weighting or scoring data. Complex 
problems such as network robustness cannot be simplified because of the intricate and domain-specific nature 
of tasks. Through experiments, we find that in solving complex problems such as network robustness, current 
LLMs can only design simple operations on nodes or links because they are only provided with general prompts 
and not domain knowledge; they cannot make deep use of domain knowledge to design advanced operations. To 
ovcome this, the network optimization strategies are designed with the intrinsic properties of networks in mind 
to guide LLMs in designing sophisticated heuristics.
Network optimization strategies
NOSs utilize network features such as degree distributions, clustering coefficients, centrality measures, and path 
characteristics to inform heuristic design. These features inspire strategies like prioritizing high-degree nodes, 
prioritizing betweenness centrality or optimizing shortest paths, which guide actions such as adding, rewiring, 
or swapping edges. This combination of features, strategies, and actions forms the NOS. The details are provided 
in Appendix B.
Variation operations
In AutoRNet, three variation operations–E1, M1, and M2–are designed to generate offspring heuristics by 
embedding NOSs in LLM prompts. E1 and M1 use 12 randomly selected NOSs with general prompts, while M2 
relies on general prompts alone. Table 1 summarizes their roles.
• E1 (Exploration with NOS Integration): E1 prompts the LLM with two parent heuristics and 12 random NOSs 
to generate diverse offspring, fostering innovation and escaping local optima.
• M1 (Guided Modification with NOSs): M1 refines heuristics by prompting the LLM with NOSs, enabling tar-
geted improvements through domain-guided local search.
• M2 (Local Adjustment): M2 prompts the LLM for minor heuristic tweaks without NOSs, optimizing perfor -
mance via pure local search.
Each generation employs tournament selection to allocate individuals to E1, M1, and M2 based on probabilities 
pe1, pm1, and pm2, selecting pe1 × popsize, pm1 × popsize, and pm2 × popsize individuals, respectively. 
Prompts are processed concurrently by the LLM server, enhancing efficiency. Figure 3 illustrates the M1 prompt 
schematic, with E1 and M2 details in Appendix C. These operations collectively balance exploration and 
refinement, optimizing network robustness.
Experiments
AutoRNet generates heuristic methods, and we analyse these methods to illustrate the design capabilities of 
AutoRNet. During the evolutionary phase, heuristics are evaluated on smaller-scale graphs to compute fitness 
scores efficiently, and in the testing phase, the finalized heuristics are applied to larger graphs, including real-
world networks, to assess their generalizability and compare them against baseline algorithms. We select three 
existing algorithms as baselines: the hill climbing algorithm (HC) 27, the simulated annealing algorithm (SA) 6, 
and the smart rewiring algorithm (SR) 28. R is used to evaluate the robustness of the networks in the test graph 
set after optimization by all the algorithms. By comparing their network robustness values, we assess the 
effectiveness of the methods generated by AutoRNet.

Computational demands
AutoRNet’s reliance on LLMs and repeated fitness evaluations results in notable computational demands during 
the training phase. Heuristic generation involves 10 LLM calls per generation for a population of 10 individuals 
across 50 generations, totaling 500 calls using GPT-4 Turbo. These calls are processed concurrently, with each 
batch of 10 calls generating Python heuristics (e.g., edge swap logic) from a prompt with NOSs, processing 
approximately 400 tokens (250 input, 150 output) and taking 5 seconds on average per batch. This results in a 
total heuristic generation time of 250 seconds (4.2 minutes) across 50 generations. As GPT-4 Turbo is accessed 
remotely via an API, local resource usage for heuristic generation is minimal, with computational costs driven 
by the remote service. The estimated token cost for 500 calls (200,000 tokens) is $3.50, based on API pricing of 
$0.01 per 1000 input tokens and $0.03 per 1000 output tokens as detailed in Table 2.
Fitness evaluations based on R are performed locally. For the training phase, each of the 24 BA networks 
(50 and 100 nodes) undergoes 100 attempts per heuristic. Each individual’ s evaluations, processed concurrently 
across the 24 graphs, take approximately 0.5 seconds, and with 10 individuals per generation processed 
concurrently, each generation requires approximately 12 seconds, leveraging parallel processing across 32 cores. 
Across 50 generations, this results in 600 seconds (10 minutes) for fitness evaluations per training run. The

Evaluation results
Design capabilities of AutoRNet
After running AutoRNet for 50 generations, we select three highly valuable heuristics based on their fitness 
values, named Heuristic-v1, Heuristic-v2 and Heuristic-v3, which significantly improve the network robustness 
of the test graph set. Heuristic-v1 leverages the network features of critical nodes and similar nodes, Heuristic-v2 
utilizes the node connectivity and degree distribution, and Heuristic-v3 optimizes the local topology by 
manipulating edges among neighbours. All three heuristics maintain the same number of edges, and Heuristic-v1 
also preserves the degree distribution. This demonstrates AutoRNet’s ability to design complete algorithms that 
effectively utilize network features.
Heuristic-v1, shown in Algorithm 2, employs an advanced strategy that combines edge swapping with SA 
while preserving the degree distribution. This heuristic identifies critical nodes (those with the highest degrees) 
and pairs similar nodes on the basis of their degree deviation. It dynamically adjusts the max_diff parameter 
(Step 4), which controls the tolerance for degree deviation in pairing similar nodes (Steps 22–34). The algo-
rithm iteratively swaps edges between the neighbours of these paired nodes (Steps 18–19), evaluating new con-
figurations on the basis of robustness improvements or probabilistic acceptance criteria derived from simulated 
annealing (Step 22). This sophisticated approach ensures a balance between exploration and exploitation in the 
search space. Without NOSs, it would be impossible to evolve such “intelligent” heuristics to leverage network 
features for robust network design. 
Algorithm 2. Heuristic-v1: Enhancing Network Robustness via simulated Annealing and Edge Swaps 
Heuristic-v2 and Heuristic-v3 optimize the network through edge relocation, which, while slightly altering 
the degree distribution, significantly improves the robustness of the networks. Importantly, edge-relocation 
actions incur the same real-world costs as edge-swapping actions. This shows that AutoRNet is not constrained 
by theoretical conditions but instead explores a variety of methods, making it more suitable for solving real-world 
problems.
Heuristic-v2, shown in Algorithm 3, improves the network robustness by redistributing edges between high-
degree and low-degree nodes. This heuristic involves adding an edge between a high-degree node and a low-
degree node while removing an edge from the high-degree node.
Heuristic-v3, shown in Algorithm 4, increases robustness by redistributing edges among the neighbours of 
high-degree nodes. This method identifies high-degree nodes and strategically adds edges between their neigh-
bours, maintaining the overall edge count but improving resilience to failure.

Impact of the AFF and temperature
In traditional EAs, the fitness function directly evaluates solutions within a constrained search space. However, in 
AutoRNet, the search space consists of heuristics rather than direct solutions, meaning that the fitness evaluation 
depends on the effectiveness of the generated heuristics rather than the solutions themselves. This fundamental 
difference affects how fitness evolves over time.
Figure 4 illustrates this effect. In the absence of the AFF , the fitness function strictly enforces degree 
distribution constraints at all stages. Consequently, the generated heuristics remain confined to the initial seed 
heuristic, which primarily performs random edge exchanges. This lack of exploration results in a negligible 
fitness improvement over the generations, leading to a nearly flat fitness curve.
When the AFF is introduced, the fitness function initially relaxes the constraints, allowing for a broader 
exploration of the heuristic space. This leads to a rapid increase in fitness as the generated heuristics become more 
effective in enhancing network robustness. However, as evolution progresses, the AFF progressively strengthens 
the constraints, guiding the heuristics towards solutions that fully satisfy the degree distribution requirements. 
This constraint tightening results in a gradual decline in fitness, as heuristics that were previously effective under 
relaxed constraints become less viable under stricter conditions. Eventually, the process stabilizes, with fitness 
converging to a final plateau at which the heuristics maintain both robustness and structural feasibility. As shown 
in Fig. 4, the performance of AutoRNet gradually converges after 40 generations. Since increasing the number of 
generations will raise the cost of calling LLMs, we have set the number of generations to 50.
With respect to the temperature parameter, we evaluated two settings: 1.0 and 0.5. While both configurations 
ultimately produced heuristics of comparable effectiveness, a temperature of 1.0 led to faster fitness improvement 
in the early generations. This suggests that increasing the temperature, by increasing the diversity of LLM-
generated heuristics, enhances exploration and accelerates convergence towards high-quality heuristics. As the 
constraints tighten in later generations, both temperature settings converge to similar results, indicating that 
the initial randomness aids exploration, whereas the final strict constraints ensure adherence to the structural 
constraints.
These observations highlight a fundamental characteristic of AutoRNet: fitness evolution is influenced 
by heuristic exploration rather than direct solution improvement . Unlike traditional EAs, the fitness of which 
typically increases monotonically, the observed nonmonotonic fitness progression in Fig. 4 is a result of the 
AFF’s dynamic constraint enforcement. This underscores the importance of balancing heuristic diversity in the 
early generations with structural feasibility in the later generations to achieve optimal results.
Limitations and future research directions
While AutoRNet demonstrates significant advancements in generating heuristics for network robustness 
optimization, it faces several limitations that highlight opportunities for future research.
A primary limitation is the reliance on manually curated NOSs and domain-specific prompts, which 
require expert knowledge to define network properties. While AutoRNet automates heuristic code generation, 
the quality of these heuristics depends on the expertise embedded in NOSs and prompts, limiting the extent 
of automation. Another challenge is the computational overhead of using high-capacity LLMs for heuristic 
generation and repeated fitness evaluations. This is particularly pronounced for large-scale networks, where 
computational costs can be significant. Additionally, the potential variability in LLM outputs poses a risk of 
inconsistent or suboptimal heuristics. Although AutoRNet mitigates this through generating multiple heuristic 
candidates to ensure diversity, variability may still impact heuristic quality in certain cases. Finally, AutoRNet is 
designed specifically for scale-free network robustness optimization, and its applicability to other combinatorial 
optimization problems, such as scheduling or clustering, remains untested, limiting its demonstrated scope.
Future research can address these limitations through several promising directions. To reduce dependency 
on manual prompt design, automated prompt evolution techniques, such as prompt optimization 30,31 or 
Retrieval-Augmented Generation 32, could dynamically generate or refine prompts or retrieved domain 
knowledge, minimizing expert intervention. To enhance computational efficiency, model compression 
techniques, including pruning33 and knowledge distillation34, can reduce the computational footprint of LLMs 
while preserving performance. Surrogate models35 could approximate the robustness measure R, reducing the 
need for expensive fitness evaluations, and distributed computing 36 can parallelize the evolutionary process to 
improve scalability for larger networks. T o address LLM output instability and lack of transparency, enhancing 
explainability and reasoning capabilities through techniques like chain-of-thought prompting 37, Self-Taught 
Reasoner (STaR)38–40, and explainable AI (XAI) methods 41,42 could improve the consistency, reliability, and 
interpretability of generated heuristics. XAI techniques can provide insights into why specific optimization 
strategies are produced, enabling error diagnosis and refinement, while STaR’ s iterative reasoning can enhance 
heuristic quality by allowing LLMs to refine outputs over multiple steps. Exploring the application of AutoRNet 
to other network types, such as random or small-world networks, or broader optimization domains, could 
further validate its generalizability. These advancements would enhance AutoRNet’ s practicality, scalability, and 
robustness for real-world applications.
Conclusion
This paper presents AutoRNet, a novel framework integrating LLMs with EAs to automate the generation of 
complete heuristics for enhancing scale-free network robustness. Building on the growing field of LLM-driven 
optimization, AutoRNet reduces the reliance on time-consuming manual heuristic design by leveraging expert-

crafted NOSs and domain-specific prompts, improving efficiency for complex network problems. The approach 
employs network optimization strategy (NOS)-based variation operations to craft domain-specific LLM prompts 
and an adaptive fitness function (AFF) to relax hard constraints, improving search effectiveness. Experimental 
results demonstrate that AutoRNet designs heuristics matching manual complexity, outperforming baseline 
algorithms on synthetic networks of varying sizes and densities, and a real-world network, at comparable cost. 
These results advance knowledge by expanding automated heuristic design for real-world applications. Despite 
its advancements, AutoRNet has several limitations. These include reliance on expert-defined prompts and NOSs, 
potential variability in LLM outputs, limited generalizability to other domains, and significant computational 
costs for large networks. The framework relies on high-quality LLM responses, which may introduce variability 
if the LLM generates suboptimal or inconsistent heuristics. Additionally, the computational cost of fitness 
evaluations, particularly for large networks, can be significant due to repeated calls to R. Scaling AutoRNet to 
very large real-world networks may require substantial resources, and the lack of transparency in LLM-generated 
heuristics poses challenges for understanding their decision-making processes. Addressing these challenges 
through automated prompt optimization, model compression, and broader evaluations, including its potential 
application to diverse network topologies or optimization problems, will be critical for enhancing AutoRNet’s 
practicality and robustness.
